## Database Recommendation

For a healthcare startup building a patient management system, MySQL would be the more appropriate choice. Healthcare data is highly sensitive and requires strict guarantees of accuracy, consistency, and durability. MySQL, as a relational database, adheres to ACID principles (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability). This ensures that patient records—such as medical histories, prescriptions, and lab results—are updated reliably and consistently across the system. In healthcare, even a minor inconsistency could lead to serious consequences, so strong transactional integrity is essential. MySQL’s structured schema, foreign keys, and relational joins also make it well‑suited for modeling complex relationships between patients, doctors, appointments, and treatments.

By contrast, MongoDB follows a BASE philosophy (Basically Available, Soft state, Eventually consistent). It offers flexibility with schema‑less design and horizontal scalability, which is valuable for rapidly changing or unstructured data. However, BASE trades off strong consistency for availability and partition tolerance under the CAP theorem. In healthcare, where accuracy and reliability outweigh flexibility, this trade‑off introduces risk. A patient management system cannot afford eventual consistency when dealing with critical medical information.

That said, if the system also needs to incorporate a fraud detection module, the recommendation may evolve. Fraud detection often involves analyzing large volumes of semi‑structured or rapidly changing data, such as logs, transactions, or behavioral patterns. MongoDB’s flexible schema and ability to scale horizontally make it attractive for this analytical workload. In such a scenario, a polyglot architecture could be ideal: MySQL for core patient records (ensuring ACID compliance) and MongoDB for fraud detection (leveraging scalability and flexible document storage). This hybrid approach balances reliability with adaptability, aligning each database’s strengths to the specific needs of the system.
