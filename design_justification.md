## Storage Systems
For patient readmission prediction, I selected a **relational data warehouse** (PostgreSQL/SQL Server) to store structured treatment records. This ensures clean, normalized data for AI model training. For natural language queries by doctors, a **document database** (MongoDB) stores unstructured notes and histories, paired with an NLP engine. Monthly management reports are generated from an **OLAP warehouse** (Snowflake/BigQuery) optimized for aggregations. ICU vitals streaming is handled by a **time-series database** (InfluxDB) or Kafka + cloud object storage, enabling real-time ingestion and historical replay.

## OLTP vs OLAP Boundary
The OLTP boundary lies at the **Electronic Health Record (EHR)** system, where patient admissions, treatments, and billing are captured. Once data is extracted and transformed, it moves into OLAP systems for analytics. OLTP ensures immediate consistency for patient care, while OLAP provides aggregated historical views for management and predictive analytics. The ETL pipeline marks the transition point between transactional and analytical systems.

## Trade-offs
A key trade-off is balancing **real-time streaming with compliance and reliability**. ICU vitals demand low-latency ingestion, but healthcare regulations require secure, auditable storage. Kafka or time-series DBs provide speed but add governance complexity. To mitigate this, I recommend layering a **secure data lakehouse** on top of streaming ingestion, enforcing schema, encryption, and access controls. Another trade-off is cost: maintaining multiple specialized systems (warehouse, document DB, time-series DB) increases overhead. Mitigation involves adopting a cloud-native Lakehouse (Databricks/Snowflake with streaming connectors) that unifies structured and unstructured data, reducing duplication and operational burden.
